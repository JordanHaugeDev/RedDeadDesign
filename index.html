The Design and Narrative of

Red Dead Redemption II

by Jordan Hauge

Introduction

Red Dead Redemption II is an open-world western action-adventure game developed by Rockstar Games, released in October of 2018. The game takes place in the fictionalized Wild West of 1899. The world is changing rapidly, almost too fast for the group the game follows. They are the Van Der Linde Gang, a group of outlaws struggling to survive in an ever-changing country and sticking firmly to their outlaw way of life. As the world around them becomes more civilized and law enforcement tightens its grip, the gang begins to face more turmoil than ever. The protagonist, Arthur Morgan, has to decide what kind of man he’s going to be as his family falls apart around him and his health quickly declines.

2018 was an incredible year for gaming, but something about RDR2 stood out amongst the rest, despite having steep competition from game of the year winner God of War and a plethora of other titles. Besides being a visually stunning sequel to the critically acclaimed Red Dead Redemption (2010), the game had something to it, there was a buzz around it. I haven’t fully shaken it. I still make references to the game 6 years later with friends and family. Having finished my 3rd playthrough just a few months ago, I have firmly placed the game in my top 3 of all time. It’s the balance the game achieves that does it for me. Sure, there are other games with better combat, better traversal, a better original score, a better this, and a better that, but where RDR2 excels for me is the harmony of all its parts. Every aspect of the game delivers and works together as a part of a whole. The world is filled with meticulous detail and diverse environments. The characters are complex and clearly defined. The story is filled with themes of loyalty, betrayal, and redemption. The gameplay mechanics are balanced and robust. The performances are heartfelt and lived in. It all comes together to create a living, breathing, immersive world that marries its components seamlessly.

The Sandbox

Take a look at the RDR2 interactive map: here

While RDR2 offers an excellent story for players to work their way through, its open world is equally impressive. After a nearly three-hour story prologue, the player is finally cut loose, free to explore the open world of this fictionalized version of the wild west. The environments range from snow ridden mountain tops, to dense forests, to wide open pastures, and sizzling deserts. The world has a full day night cycle and dynamic weather events. The animals graze and hunt for food. Towns are bustling with civilians going about their days, each person can be interacted with in a positive or negative way. Buildings and houses are being built and strangers in need are in every corner of the map. This world gives the player the sense that if you just left it alone, it would live on uninterrupted. It feels alive and it’s incredibly immersive.

The open-world design and player agency foster emergent gameplay experiences. Players can stray from the main story and stumble upon unexpected events, like a shootout between rival gangs or a lone traveler needing help. These unscripted moments contribute to the feeling of a living world and provide unique experiences for each player. Domsch talks about Timed Events and the Intradiegetic Clock. RDR2 takes full advantage of the in-game time, having many events only available at certain times of day. Missions with the mayor of Saint Denis are only available at night while other stranger missions are only available during the day. Many of the main quests can be done at any time of day and will reflect as such during the mission and cutscenes.

A core tenet of Piccuci's sandbox philosophy is that player agency fosters emergent storytelling. The idea of motivating players to explore the world and uncover new secrets or characters is the backbone of the game's world design. This lets the player create their own stories out in the world and lets them tackle objectives in a nonlinear fashion. The freedom allowed in the sandbox is vast. Most non-player characters are able to be interacted with by either greeting or antagonizing. Nearly all non-player characters can be killed, including some of the strangers who give missions like William the herb enthusiast. Strangers in need can be found during travel, whether it be a man caught in a bear trap, a woman whose horse collapsed, or a stagecoach being robbed by a rival gang. Giving players this much freedom allows them to choose what kind of outlaw they want to be, whether they want to be friendly or want to feed civilians to gators, it's up to them. Sandbox games like RDR2, "are designed to maximize the paidia type of play and provide an interacive environment where players can exercise creativity and craft individual stories which maintain a sense of cohesiveness within the greater narrative framework" (Piccuci 110).

Rival gangs are everywhere. The O'Driscolls, Murfrees, Laramies, Skinners, Lemoyne Raiders, etc, can be found setting up camp and ambushing civilians throughout the open world. They even search and ambush the main camp if the player isn't careful. The non-scripted nature of these moments can lead to genuine surprises and unique experiences for each player. That being said, these events are not without logic. Each gang has their territory, for example you wont run into the Skinners unless you are out West near BlackWater and are unlikely to run into Lemoyne Raiders unless out East just outside Saint Denis. Knowing the territory means knowing more of what to expect the world to throw at you.

The pacing and delivery of the narratives in the sandbox are up to the player, "unlike the Pre-established structure, here we are presented with a higher degree of nonlinearity combined with the frequent presence of side-stories and/or multiple story branches. While an overarching pre established storyline is still present, more space is given to the exploration of the environments and optional retrieval of additional story information" (Piccuci 107).

Gameplay

Players can traverse the vast open world on horseback or by foot. The game utilizes a third-person perspective, allowing you to take in the scenery and interact with the environment. Players can engage in gunfights using a variety of period-appropriate weapons like pistols, rifles, and shotguns. A targeting system assists with aiming, but manual aiming rewards skill. Dead Eye, a bullet-time mechanic, allows for rapid takedowns of multiple enemies. Hand-to-hand combat adds another layer, requiring strategy and quick reflexes. The player's horse isn't just a mode of transportation, it's also a loyal companion. Bonding with the horse through brushing, feeding, and calming increases its manueverability, speed, and loyalty. Challenges can be made or discovered by the player. The game encourages this through rewards and achievements. An example is an achievement for having a bounty of $250 in every state at the same time. Other challenges like horse challenges or gunslinger challenges can lead to rewards that include clothing items and pouches. Much of the fun is in setting challenges for yourself, like hunting for 3 star pelts or seeing how long you can last in a shootout with the law in Saint Denis.

Feedback is given in a number of ways, through hit markers, stamina, health, and dead eye cores, as well as the HUD. Hit markers will change color based on whether the shot was a critical hit or not and the players honor status will be represented through many of their actions. The game has a bit of a learning curve for the mechanics; It is not uncommon to accidentally shoot a stranger when you were trying to greet them. Though with time and practice, mastery of the mechanics can lead to incredibly satisfying combat and traversal.

Mods give way to a plethora of new game modes, features, and emergent gameplay. Trainers, weapons, and cosmetic items previously exclusive to Red Dead Online have made their way to the single player experience through modding. Custom mods allow players to dress up as Clint Eastwood, John Wick, or even Red Harlow from Red Dead Revolver. With mods, the gameplay mechanics can be modified to your heart's content. One of my favorites in Undead Nightmare II, a mod that takes inspiration from the first game's bonus zombie mode. While the mod doesn't have a story, it is a lot of fun to see the world transformed into an apocalyptic wasteland and get a glimpse at what could have been if Rockstar decided to make a sequel to the highly popular original mode from 2010.

Interactivity

Unlike generic background characters, the NPCs in RDR2 offer a variety of interactive possibilities. Players can greet them with a simple "Howdy" or engage in deeper conversations that reveal hidden stories or lead to side quests. These interactions aren't limited to dialogue. Players can offer help to those in need, gamble in saloons, or even rob unsuspecting townsfolk (though the consequences will vary depending on Arthur's honor rating). This range of interaction makes the world feel like it's populated by real people, not pre-programmed NPCs.

The game's interactivity extends to the environment itself and the space of possibility is enormous. Players can not only admire the scenery, but they can interact with it in meaningful ways. Hunting animals for food and crafting materials, fishing for relaxation, or simply picking herbs for medicine all add to the feeling of inhabiting a living world. These interactions are further enhanced by diegetic systems. Brushing and feeding your horse isn't just a menu option; it's a process that strengthens the bond between player and mount. These details may seem small, but they contribute to a more immersive and interactive experience.

The interaction between player and nature seems to have been a big focus in Rockstar's development process. The wildlife responds realistically to the player's actions and seems to have a functioning ecosystem. While exploration, hunting, and combat are core aspects of the game, interactivity extends beyond them. Players can engage in a variety of mini-games, from poker and blackjack in saloons to five finger filet and challenges with other characters. These activities offer a welcome break from the main story and further enrich the world-building and characters.

Much of the big decisions in the story are examples of Salen and Zimmerman's third mode of interactivity, Explicit interactivity. These are moments in the story where the cutscene takes a pause to let the player reflect on their current decision and react accordingly. This interaction is normally through the press of button 1 or button 2, but these choices stack up throughout the game and determine what kind of man Arthur is. Agency in the game's main story is often a bit limited, being a choice between good or bad, (low or high honor). The outcomes to these choices are obvious, so a player who wants to lean into the idea of Arthur being redeemed will likely always select the choice that gives positive honor, making the selection of narrative choices a bit slim. But the fact that the game has any agency at all considering the scale and depth of the world is incredible in itself.

Honor

high

A high honor Arthur is seen favorably by the citizens of the Wild West. This translates to significant discounts in shops throughout the game world. Whether you're looking for new weapons, provisions, or stylish clothing, a high honor rating ensures you get a better deal. Additionally, certain shops and missions might even be inaccessible to low honor outlaws. Maintaining a good reputation opens doors literally and metaphorically in the world. High honor isn't just about discounts and quests, it can also make the core gameplay loop more enjoyable. Helping people and completing honorable missions often yield positive emotional rewards. Witnessing the gratitude of those you've helped can be a satisfying experience, adding a layer of purpose to your actions while also progressing Arthur's character arc.

low

The world rewards the ruthless. Low honor opens doors to lucrative opportunities like robbing banks, trains, and unsuspecting travelers. These risky endeavors can yield significant financial gains, allowing you to acquire better weapons, gear, and horses. Low honor also generally increases the amount of loot you find on dead bodies, further fueling the outlaw playstyle. The freedom of action no matter the consequences is something players often enjoy in gaming. Games like Grand Theft Auto or Postal give players an open playground to go nuts in. This playstyle can be a refreshing change of pace for players looking for a more action-oriented experience.

The honor system is the game's main way of expressing agency throughout the story and the sandbox. The game will react to your choices and adjust character interactions and story beats accordingly. While there are a number of choices to be made throughout the narrative, Red Dead Redemption 2's agency does have limitations. The core narrative arc exists, and many mission outcomes are predetermined. However, the order/way you approach these missions, the choices you make along the way, and the world you interact with, all contribute to a unique and memorable experience for each player.

Story

The Story follows the Van Der Linde gang led by outlaw Dutch Van Der Linde. He is a charismatic leader with dreams of living free from rules and order with his surrogate gang family. The gang operates under Dutch's idealistic vision of "one last score" that will allow them to escape the law and live freely. However, as the story progresses, it becomes clear that this dream is fading. As the gang faces mounting pressure and setbacks, tensions rise. Dutch's leadership becomes increasingly erratic and paranoid, straining his relationships with Arthur and other members. These internal conflicts threaten to tear the gang apart. Arthur struggles with his loyalty to his family and doing what's right. Arthur faces his mortality as he struggles with a new health diagnosis that puts his life on a countdown. He reflects on his past choices and his desire for redemption emerges.

Looking at the story through Schell's lens of the Hero's Journey, Arthur goes through many of the steps and can be seen as going from a selfish outlaw to a man who just wants to do some good with the time he has left. The story follows the classic archetypes, the call to adventure, the challenges and temptations, the revelation, transformation, atonement, and resolution. The journey begins with the gang in the thick of it, and they really never get out of it. A shootout in BlackWater has forced the gang into constant moving and hiding. Along his journey Arthur meets a number of people who inspire him, including a Native American named Rain Falls and a nun named Calderon. As the rift between himself and Dutch grows he begins to see things more clearly and his transformation begins. He reconciles with his past mistakes and begins to make things right in his own way. In the end, his redemption comes from trying to make other's lives better rather than his own. Arthur discovers the good in himself even if he denies its presence. His character arc is one of intrinsic growth motivated by a sudden death sentence that gives him clarity. Some may argue he isn't a hero, but the game definitely wants you to consider that he can be, especially with some of the choices the player can make towards the end. Do you help John get back to his family and escape the attack, or do you go back to the cave for the stockpile of money? The degree of his heroic nature and his redemption is up to the player.

Narrative Design

RDR2 strikes a balance between Piccuci's idea of pre-established narrative structure and discovery narrative. The player can weave their story between these two narrative delivery methods. Each mission in the game more or less has a definitive ending, though the order you tackle them is almost entirely up to you. In each act the map is populated with multiple quest markers, usually starting with the quest giver's first initial. Whether the player wants to do a Dutch mission first or a Sadie mission first or any other available mission is entirely up to them. When in a mission, the structure is largely linear. Rockstar likely focused on a pre-established narrative because they had a specific story they wanted the players to experience, rather than creating branching paths. Because of this, missions often don't affect other available missions, but completing them does normally populate the map with new ones.

In many ways the pacing of the game could be considered a slow burn, but this only allows the player to become more invested in the story and the characters. Spending more time at camp and interacting with the story's main characters gives a deeper appreciation to what each character's beliefs/values are and what obstacles are in their way. Seeing the group as a family makes the story all the more tragic as well as bittersweet. It's really a narrative about a family breaking apart in the most dramatic of ways.

The importance of character cannot be overstated here. Miller says, "One of the truly unique aspects of digital characters, as opposed to characters created for movies or traditional TV, is that you don’t just watch them; you can actually be them. Or, at the very least, you can interact with them" (Miller 90). There's something about being in a character's shoes that makes you appreciate the situation all the more. In a game like The Last of Us, being Joel is far more stressful than just watching Joel. Shifting perspectives changes everything. When you play as Ellie near the end of The Last of Us I, it's scarier than playing as Joel, the sense of vulnerability is increased. The same goes for playing Arthur's story rather than just watching it. When you experience the diagnosis after playing as him for so long, it feels like heartbreaking news and suddenly you realize the game is coming to an end, and if you're thoroughly enjoying it like I did, that's bad news. Perspective makes all the difference in storytelling.

Miller asks, "Is it essential to include an oppositional character or series of them in every work of digital entertainment?" (Miller 96). The simple answer is no, antagonists are not essential, but they are compelling. Part of what makes being Arthur so stressful is the amount of Antagonists he has to deal with. Not only are there a number of rival gangs out to get the Van Der Lindes, but he eventually has to worry about his closest friends and family. Dutch slowly becomes the antagonist over the course of the narrative and since he is a father-figure and a brother to Arthur, it makes it all the more painful. Dutch is a complex antagonist because he never really had ill intentions towards his family, he just couldn't achieve his dream and the constant setbacks as well as head trauma lead to his increasing paranoia and loss of control. He brought in all these people that the world rejected and created a family. He is the first one to step up when an outsider needs help, but his ability to rein himself in dies with Hosea and Molly. He is also poisoned by Micah, one of the newest members of the gang who has a lack of self control and a faulty moral compass. Micah can be seen as the true antagonist of the game because of his shady nature and deep betrayal. In many ways he is the one who tears the group apart and the game sets him up to be the final conflict of the epilogue.

Taking on all these challenges and storylines can take the player well over 60+ hours; with all the side quests and sandbox activities, achieving 100% completion can take well over 120+ hours. The design of the narrative is extremely well thought out and carefully crafted. The ability the player has to weave in and out of the narrative and control the pace of the story makes it one of the greatest open-world action adventure narratives to date.

Conclusion

There are plennty of games with much deeper agency and player choice, but where RDR2 lacks in meaningful decision making, it makes up for in compelling narrative and a rich open-world. The mechanics are robust and the performances are award worthy. Roger Clark, Benjamin Byron Davis, Rob Wiethoff, Alex McKenna, Peter Blomquist, and the rest of the incredible cast put over 5 years into performance capture/voice work and deepened my appreciation for video game performances in a big way. While the game has drawbacks as many do, this game is a masterpiece in my mind. It finds a way to balance the heart of the story, with a GTA style sandbox all while making it feel cohesive.

If you asked me to show someone who never plays video games an example of why the medium is so beloved, this is the one I would show them. The visuals alone are breathtaking, but the deeper you look, the more this game has to offer. In many ways it almost feels like an adaptation of a book and I mean that in the best way possible. I play a lot of games, but outside of pure nostalgia, this game has stuck with me in a way many haven't. As I said, it isn't the best at any one thing necessarily, but the balance it strikes is awe inspiring and I can only imagine the depth of the development process.

Arthur will stick with me as one of my favorite Protagonists of any medium, along with Kratos, Walter White, and Jimmy McGill. There's something about flawed characters that resonates with audiences (& me) in a deep way. These are characters who make mistakes and often end up paying for them in big ways. But these are also characters who seek Redemption as well. The idea behind characters like these is that a person can change and is the sum of the actions they make going forward, not just the ones they made in their past. These stories belong in any medium, but I think gaming offers a different perspective and that's what excites me about the future of it more than any other medium. Games are advancing at an incredible rate and the nature/potential of telling great stories is only getting better. Red Dead Redemption II will stand out as one of the greatest examples of what the medium can offer and will be remembered and played for decades to come.
